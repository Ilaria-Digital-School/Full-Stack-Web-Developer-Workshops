# Workshop: writing meaningful commits

**Theoretical Recap** A commit is more than a save point — it is a unit of meaning in your project's history. A meaningful commit answers three questions implicitly: What changed? Why did it change? And is this change isolated and coherent? The golden rule is: one commit = one logical change. This means you should not bundle a bug fix, a feature addition, and a typo correction into a single commit. Each concern deserves its own entry in history. A well-written commit message follows a simple structure: a short imperative subject line (50 characters max), optionally followed by a blank line and a more detailed body explaining the why, not the what. Write 'Fix broken navigation link in header' rather than 'fixed stuff' or 'update'. The imperative mood ('Fix', 'Add', 'Remove', 'Refactor') is a convention aligned with how Git itself writes auto-generated messages. Common mistakes include vague messages like 'WIP', 'misc', or 'changes'; committing too rarely (resulting in giant, unreadable diffs); committing too often with meaningless micro-saves; and mixing unrelated changes. A clean commit history is a form of documentation — future you, and your future teammates, will read it to understand the evolution of a codebase.
